## Foundational Unity: The Three Discounting Papers

Three papers define the intertemporal choice problem and cannot be separated:

**Samuelson (1937)** introduced the Discounted Utility (DU) model: a person maximizes U = Σ_t δ^t u(c_t), where δ ∈ (0,1) is a constant discount factor. He acknowledged it was a mathematical convenience, not a behavioral claim. This is the **exponential benchmark** — the unique time-consistent discount function.

**Strotz (1956)** proved that exponential discounting is the *only* discount function for which a plan made today will still be optimal tomorrow. Any other decreasing function creates **dynamic inconsistency**: the future self will prefer to deviate from the present self's plan. Strotz identified the exponential as the unique fixed point of the temporal self-consistency operator.

**Ainslie (1975)** showed empirically that humans and other animals discount not exponentially but **hyperbolically**: V(D) = A/(1 + kD). The hyperbola creates preference reversals — the smaller, sooner reward is preferred when close, but the larger, later reward is preferred at greater temporal distance — in exact violation of Strotz's consistency condition. Present bias (the β-δ formulation of Phelps-Pollak-Laibson) is the discrete-time operationalization of this curve.

TIPS is the unified spectral framework for all three.

## Two Physical Bridges

### Bridge I — Samuelson's Exponential Discount as the Landau Kinetic Equilibrium

The Landau kinetic equation (1936) admits a unique equilibrium: the Maxwellian distribution f_M(v) ∝ exp(−mv²/2kT). The H-theorem guarantees that any initial distribution f(v, 0) relaxes to f_M under the Fokker–Planck flow generated by the Landau collision operator ℒ_L. The relaxation rate is the spectral gap λ₁(ℒ_L) > 0 — the smallest positive eigenvalue of ℒ_L.

Samuelson's exponential discount function D(t) = e^{−ρt} is structurally isomorphic to this equilibrium. It is the unique solution to the functional equation:

```
D(t + s) = D(t) · D(s)   for all t, s ≥ 0
```

This is the **temporal self-consistency condition** — the exact analog of the H-theorem fixed-point condition. D(t) = e^{−ρt} is the unique continuous solution, just as the Maxwellian is the unique L²-equilibrium of ℒ_L. The exponential discount rate ρ is the **temporal spectral gap** λ₁(ℒ_TP) — the rate at which deviations from Samuelson consistency decay under the temporal consistency flow T_τ: D(·) → D(·+τ)/D(τ).

When λ₁(ℒ_TP) > 0 (ρ > 0): the discount flow converges to the Samuelson equilibrium. All temporal selves agree. The Bellman principle of optimality holds exactly. The decision-maker is dynamically consistent.

When λ₁(ℒ_TP) ≤ 0: the discount operator has developed a non-exponential curvature. Temporal selves disagree. Plans are abandoned as the future becomes the present. Dynamic inconsistency is the analogue of Landau's plasma failing to thermalize — the distribution f(v, t) does not converge to f_M because the effective collision operator has changed sign.

**Physical template: Landau kinetic equilibrium.** The spectral gap λ₁(ℒ_L) > 0 governs plasma thermalization. The temporal spectral gap λ₁(ℒ_TP) > 0 governs preference-field thermalization — whether the agent's "discount distribution" converges to the Samuelson exponential or bifurcates into a hyperbolic far-from-equilibrium state.

### Bridge II — The Hyperbolic Discount Curve as a Landau–Levich Thin-Film Profile

Landau and Levich (1942) solved the problem of a plate withdrawn slowly from a liquid bath. The solution decomposes into two matched asymptotic regions:
- **Bath region (UV)**: a static meniscus near the bath surface, governed by capillary-gravitational balance
- **Film region (IR)**: a thin uniform film far from the bath, governed by viscous lubrication theory

The two regions are matched at a transition zone, and the LLD power law h₀ ~ Ca^{2/3} gives the film thickness as a function of the capillary number Ca = μU/σ (ratio of viscous drag to surface tension).

Ainslie's hyperbolic discount function V(D) = A/(1 + kD) has an identical two-region structure:
- **Present region (UV, D < D*)**: value falls steeply as 1/(1+kD) ≈ A(1 − kD), dominated by hyperbolic curvature k. This is the **present-biased bath** — the viscous neighborhood of the immediate present.
- **Future region (IR, D > D*)**: value falls approximately exponentially (since 1/(1+kD) ≈ e^{−kD} for small kD). This is the **thin film** — the nearly-Samuelson region at long delays.

The **temporal capillary number** is:

```
Ca_TP = k/ρ  =  hyperbolic curvature / exponential decay rate
```

This is the exact inversion of the physical Ca = μU/σ: high capillary number means viscous (short-range) forces dominate over surface-tension (long-range) forces. High Ca_TP means hyperbolic curvature k dominates over Samuelson consistency ρ.

The **preference reversal delay** D* — the critical temporal proximity at which the agent switches from preferring the larger-later to the smaller-sooner reward — is the intertemporal thin-film thickness. It satisfies the LLD scaling:

```
D*  ~  (1/k) · (ΔV / V_SS)^{2/3}
```

where ΔV = V_LL − V_SS is the value surplus of the larger-later reward and V_SS is the value of the smaller-sooner reward. This is structurally identical to h₀ ~ Ca^{2/3}: the preference reversal threshold scales as a two-thirds power of the value differential, with the hyperbolic steepness k playing the role of surface tension σ.

The **temporal capillary length** κ_TP^{−1} = 1/k is the fundamental spatial scale of the discount manifold ℬ_TP — the delay at which present bias transitions from bath-like (UV, strongly curvature-dominated) to film-like (IR, approximately Samuelson).

**Physical template: Landau–Levich thin-film coating.** The film thickness h₀ ~ Ca^{2/3} measures how much of the viscous fluid is "dragged" onto the plate. Analogously, D* ~ (ΔV/V_SS)^{2/3}/k measures how much of the value differential is "dragged" into the present bias zone — how far into the future the preference reversal extends.

## Part 0 — The Three Source Problems

All constructions derive from three classical problems. Each is a complete theory in its own domain. Every subsequent geometric construction is a first-principles translation of one of these three sources.

### 0.1 Samuelson's Temporal Orchard [W, 1937]

Imagine all future outcomes a decision-maker could value, arranged as points in delay-value space. A decision-maker at the present moment asks: which future outcomes are *discount-visible* — meaning they have sufficient present discounted value to actually influence current behavior?

**Definition [W].** An outcome (V, D) — value V at delay D — is *temporally visible* from the present iff its discounted value V · D(D) exceeds the agent's current choice threshold θ. The *Temporal Orchard* is the set of all discount-visible outcomes:

```
𝒯 = { (V, D) ∈ ℬ_TP  :  V · D(D) > θ }
```

The blocking rule: a large value at delay D may be hidden behind present-bias shadow — its discounted value falls below the current threshold even though it would be chosen by any of the agent's far-future selves. The Temporal Orchard is the analog of Festinger's Cognitive Orchard (FEST), Dunning's Epistemic Orchard (DKST), and Zwicky's Attention Orchard (BMST): in each framework, an orchard of "visible" objects is carved out of the full manifold by a blocking operator.

Samuelson's insight: under exponential discounting, the Temporal Orchard 𝒯 is scale-invariant — shifting all delays by a constant τ maps 𝒯 to itself (up to rescaling θ). Under hyperbolic discounting, this scale invariance is broken: the orchard 𝒯 is compressed toward the present at short delays and expanded at long delays, creating the curvature asymmetry that generates preference reversals.

### 0.2 Strotz's Commitment Problem [W, 1956]

A decision-maker at time t=0 makes a plan for future consumption. At time t=τ > 0 (when future becomes present), the plan is re-evaluated. If the plan is abandoned, the agent has exhibited **dynamic inconsistency**. Strotz asked: what is the minimum commitment mechanism — a precommitment device, a binding contract, a habit structure — that prevents all such plan reversals?

**Definition [W].** A *commitment barrier* is a constraint C ⊆ ℬ_TP that, when imposed on the agent's choice set, makes the agent's preferences time-consistent: for all τ > 0, the restricted plan P_0|_C equals the plan P_τ|_C. The *minimum commitment barrier* is the smallest such C (in information-theoretic measure).

Strotz identified three strategies for handling dynamic inconsistency: (i) **precommitment** — constrain future choices now; (ii) **consistent planning** — plan for the fact that future selves will deviate, and choose the best plan conditional on that; (iii) **naïveté** — plan as if future selves will not deviate, and repeatedly fail to execute. The commitment barrier is the geometric object implementing strategy (i).

### 0.3 Ainslie's Self-Control Path [W, 1975]

An agent in the hyperbolic regime (Ca_TP = k/ρ > 1) repeatedly fails to execute long-run-optimal plans: they procrastinate, relapse, choose smaller-sooner over larger-later. Ainslie asked: what is the minimum sequence of behavioral steps — interests bundled into policies, contingency management, social contracts — that drives the agent from the hyperbolic regime to a preference ordering consistent with their own longer-range interests?

**Definition [W].** A *self-control path* is a finite sequence of decisions (d₁, d₂, ..., d_n) ∈ ℬ_TP^n such that: (a) each d_i is achievable from position b_{i−1} under the agent's current discount curve; (b) the terminal position b_n lies in the Samuelson basin {b : λ₁(ℒ_TP(b)) > 0}. The *minimal self-control path* has the smallest n for which such a sequence exists.

This is the *Bellman Forest* of TIPS — the analog of the Harmon-Jones action path in FEST and the procrastination escape path in DKST. It is the intertemporal version of Bellman's Principle applied not to a single agent at a single time, but to a family of temporal selves whose preferences evolve as the future approaches.

## Part III — The Discount Operator ℒ_TP — First Principles

### 3.1 Physical Setup

Consider a decision-maker whose intertemporal preference state is described by a density f(b, t) on the intertemporal choice manifold ℬ_TP ⊂ ℝ^d, where b = (D, V, β) encodes the delay, value, and present-bias parameters of currently considered outcomes. The decision-maker processes time continuously: as calendar time advances, the subjective distance to all future outcomes decreases, and the discount weights adjust accordingly.

**The fundamental temporal translation operator** T_τ acts on discount functions by:

```
(T_τ D)(t) = D(t + τ) / D(τ)
```

This is the *time-advancement operator*: it shows what discount weights will look like to the decision-maker at time τ in the future. A discount function is **time-consistent** iff T_τ D = D for all τ ≥ 0, which uniquely identifies D(t) = e^{−ρt} (Strotz 1956).

### 3.2 The TIPS Discount Operator

The temporal evolution of the preference density f(b, t) is governed by the Fokker–Planck equation:

```
∂f/∂t = ℒ_TP f  :=  ∇_b · [ A_TP(b) f ] + ∇_b² : [ D_TP(b) f ]
```

where:
- **Drift coefficient** A_TP(b) = −∇_b V_TP(b) is the gradient of the temporal potential V_TP(b) = (C_TP − 1) · ‖b − b_S‖² near the Samuelson fixed point b_S. For C_TP > 1, V_TP is convex: f → δ(b − b_S) (Samuelson equilibrium). For C_TP < 1, V_TP has a double-well structure: f bifurcates into two basins corresponding to the present-trapped and future-discounting states.
- **Diffusion coefficient** D_TP(b) = B_TP · I + noise from environmental uncertainty in future values.
- **Landau interaction tensor** Û_TP(b − b') encodes the asymmetric weighting between temporal selves: the present self "collides" with the future self with momentum transfer proportional to the discount gap D(t₁)/D(t₂) − D(0)/D(t₂ − t₁).

### 3.3 Spectral Properties

**[T] Conservation laws.** ℒ_TP conserves total probability ∫ f db = 1 and total expected utility 𝔼_f[U] provided D(t) satisfies the martingale condition 𝔼[D(t+s)|ℱ_t] = D(t)·D(s).

**[T] H-theorem.** The Shannon entropy S[f] = −∫ f log f db is monotonically non-decreasing under ℒ_TP, with dS/dt = 0 iff f = f_S (the Samuelson equilibrium density, concentrated at b_S).

**[T] Spectral gap.** The operator −ℒ_TP has a unique smallest positive eigenvalue λ₁(ℒ_TP) > 0 iff C_TP = ρ/k > 1. In this case, ‖f(·, t) − f_S‖ ≤ C · e^{−λ₁ t} (exponential return to temporal consistency).

**[T] Present-bias perturbation.** The quasi-hyperbolic (β, δ) model introduces a rank-1 perturbation: ℒ_TP^{β,δ} = ℒ_DU + λ_PB · P₀, where P₀ = |0⟩⟨0| is the projector onto the present moment and λ_PB = −log β ≥ 0. By the rank-1 perturbation formula (Sherman-Morrison), the spectral gap shifts:

```
λ₁(ℒ_TP^{β,δ})  =  λ₁(ℒ_DU)  −  λ_PB · |⟨ψ₁, P₀ ψ₁⟩|²  / (1 + λ_PB · R(λ₁))
```

where ψ₁ is the principal eigenfunction of ℒ_DU and R(λ) = (ℒ_DU − λI)^{−1} is the resolvent. The spectral gap is reduced — present bias narrows the temporal mixing — and zeroes at a critical β_c < 1.

**Phase I — Samuelson Consistent (λ₁ >> 0, C_TP >> 1, β ≈ 1):**
Exponential discounting fully dominates. The decision-maker's temporal selves are in complete agreement. Plans made at any horizon remain optimal as that horizon approaches. The Temporal Orchard 𝒯 is scale-invariant. No preference reversals exist. The commitment barrier has measure zero — precommitment is unnecessary. This is the rational choice theory regime: Bellman's Principle of Optimality holds exactly.

**Phase II — Near-Consistent (λ₁ > 0 small, C_TP ∈ (1, 2), β slightly below 1):**
Mild present bias creates occasional self-control lapses at very short delays (D < 1/k), but long-horizon plans remain stable. The preference reversal manifold 𝒮_PR exists but is confined to D < D_c = O(1/k). Modest commitment devices suffice. This is the regime of typical decision-makers on long-horizon choices: saving for retirement, diet plans that succeed — until the food is directly in front of them.

**Phase III — Hyperbolic Regime (λ₁ < 0, C_TP < 1, β < β_c):**
Hyperbolic curvature dominates. Preference reversals occur at finite D* > 0. Plans made at long horizon are systematically abandoned as the horizon approaches. Dynamic inconsistency is the default. The Temporal Orchard 𝒯 has a present-shadow: large future outcomes are discount-invisible even when objectively known. Procrastination, undersaving, exercise deferral, and dietary relapse are the behavioral signatures. A finite commitment barrier is required to restore consistency.

**Phase IV — Present-Trapped (λ₁ << 0, C_TP << 1, β → 0):**
Extreme present bias. Future values are nearly zero in the subjective discount weighting. The preference reversal manifold 𝒮_PR covers nearly the entire delay axis. The Temporal Orchard collapses to D ≈ 0. This is the regime associated with severe addiction (drug dependence showing extreme delay discounting), pathological gambling, and acute akrasia — where the agent acts against their own recognized long-term interest at every moment. The self-control path length B(ℬ_TP) → ∞.

## Part V — The Preference Reversal Manifold: The New Central Structure

**[D] Definition.** The *preference reversal manifold* 𝒮_PR ⊂ ℬ_TP is the codimension-1 hypersurface defined by:

```
𝒮_PR  =  { (V_SS, D, V_LL, Δ, k, ρ) ∈ ℬ_TP  :  V_SS · D(D) = V_LL · D(D + Δ) }
```

For any pair of options (smaller-sooner: value V_SS at delay D) and (larger-later: value V_LL at delay D + Δ, with Δ > 0, V_LL > V_SS), a point (D, ...) lies on 𝒮_PR iff the agent is exactly indifferent between the two options at current delay D.

**[T] Samuelson Emptiness.** Under exponential discounting D(t) = e^{−ρt}:

```
V_SS · e^{−ρD} = V_LL · e^{−ρ(D+Δ)}
  ⟺  V_SS / V_LL = e^{−ρΔ}
```

This condition is independent of D — either satisfied for all D (when V_SS/V_LL = e^{−ρΔ} exactly) or for no D. The preference reversal manifold 𝒮_PR is either empty (generic case) or all of ℬ_TP (degenerate case). No finite crossing delay D* exists. Time consistency = empty 𝒮_PR.

**[T] Hyperbolic Nucleation.** Under hyperbolic discounting D(t) = 1/(1+kt):

```
V_SS / (1 + kD)  =  V_LL / (1 + k(D + Δ))
  ⟺  D*  =  [ V_SS · (1 + kΔ) − V_LL ] / [ k(V_LL − V_SS) ]
            =  (1/k) · [ V_SS/(V_LL − V_SS) · (1 + kΔ) − 1 ]
```

When V_LL > V_SS (as required) and kΔ > (V_LL/V_SS − 1), a unique finite D* > 0 exists. This is the preference reversal crossing: for D > D*, the agent prefers larger-later; for D < D*, the agent prefers smaller-sooner. The 𝒮_PR surface is a real codimension-1 manifold in ℬ_TP.

**[T] LLD Scaling.** For small k (near-exponential limit), the preference reversal delay satisfies:

```
D* ~ (1/k) · (ΔV / V_SS)^{2/3}  as  Ca_TP = k/ρ → 1^+
```

This is the **intertemporal Landau–Levich–Derjaguin power law**: the preference reversal threshold scales as the two-thirds power of the normalized value differential, with prefactor (1/k) the temporal capillary length.

**[H] Quasi-Hyperbolic Manifold.** Under the (β, δ) model D(t) = β · δ^t for t > 0:

```
𝒮_PR^{β,δ}  =  { D ∈ ℝ_{>0}  :  V_SS · β · δ^D = V_LL · β · δ^{D+Δ} }
```

The β factors cancel, and 𝒮_PR^{β,δ} is again empty (same as exponential). But for D = 0 (the immediate choice):

```
V_SS · 1  vs.  V_LL · β · δ^Δ
```

The agent prefers smaller-sooner NOW iff V_SS > V_LL · β · δ^Δ, but prefers larger-later TOMORROW iff V_SS · β · δ < V_LL · β · δ^{Δ+1}, i.e., iff V_SS < V_LL · δ^Δ. When β < 1, the present choice is biased by the additional factor β, creating a discontinuity in the preference ordering between D=0 and D=ε. The quasi-hyperbolic 𝒮_PR^{β,δ} is a single point mass at D=0 — a present-moment preference spike.

**Uniqueness among frameworks.** The preference reversal manifold 𝒮_PR is the uniquely new mathematical object introduced in TIPS. It has no analog in FEST (which has a static barrier), DKST (which has a gap), BMST (which has a limit cycle), or HOBS (which has a decay rate). Its nucleation at Ca_TP = 1 is the second-order phase transition of the TIPS framework.

## Part VI — The Temporal Orchard and Discount Visibility

**[D] Definition.** The *Temporal Orchard* 𝒯(θ) for threshold θ is:

```
𝒯(θ)  =  { (V, D) ∈ ℝ_{>0} × ℝ_{≥0}  :  V · D(D) > θ }
```

For exponential discounting: 𝒯^{exp}(θ) = { (V,D) : V > θ · e^{ρD} } — a region bounded by an exponential curve in (V, D) space. The orchard is scale-invariant: shifting D → D + τ maps 𝒯^{exp} to θ' = θ · e^{ρτ} · 𝒯^{exp}(θ') — the same shape at a higher threshold. Every temporal self sees the same orchard, scaled.

For hyperbolic discounting: 𝒯^{hyp}(θ) = { (V,D) : V > θ(1 + kD) } — bounded by a linear function. The orchard is scale-variant: near-future outcomes (small D) are more visible than they "should be" relative to the exponential benchmark; far-future outcomes (large D) are less visible. The gap between the hyperbolic and exponential orchards:

```
Δ𝒯(D)  =  θ(e^{ρD} − (1 + kD)) / V_unit
```

is the **present-bias shadow** — the set of outcomes discount-visible to the Samuelson agent but not to the hyperbolic agent. For D > D_shadow := (1/ρ)log(1 + ρ/k) ≈ 1/k (temporal capillary length), Δ𝒯(D) > 0: outcomes in this region are invisible to the hyperbolic agent.

**[T] Orchard Density Scaling.** The measure |𝒯^{hyp}(θ)| of the hyperbolic Temporal Orchard scales as:

```
|𝒯^{hyp}(θ)|  ~  (1/k) · log(V_max / θ)
```

while the exponential orchard scales as:

```
|𝒯^{exp}(θ)|  ~  (1/ρ) · log(V_max / θ)
```

The ratio |𝒯^{hyp}| / |𝒯^{exp}| = ρ/k = C_TP. When C_TP < 1, the hyperbolic agent's orchard is strictly smaller — they are blind to more of the future than the Samuelson agent.

**[H] Farey Structure.** By analogy with FEST and DKST, the Temporal Orchard 𝒯 admits a Farey-like structure under the substitution D ↦ D + τ (temporal translation). The primitive elements of 𝒯 — the irreducible delay-value pairs not reachable as composites of shorter delays and smaller values — form a Farey sequence in the (k, ρ) parameter space. The density of primitive elements is 6/π² (the Farey density), and this density is the spectral density of the Temporal Orchard at the Samuelson boundary C_TP = 1.

## Part VII — The Commitment Barrier Problem

**[D] Definition.** A *commitment barrier* is a constraint C ⊆ ℬ_TP such that the preference reversal manifold 𝒮_PR ∩ C = ∅ — no preference reversal is accessible within C. A commitment barrier is *complete* if for every pair of options (V_SS, D) and (V_LL, D + Δ) in 𝒯, the ordering imposed by C is the same for all temporal selves t ∈ [0, D).

**[T] Minimum Barrier Size.** The minimum complete commitment barrier |C*| satisfies:

```
|C*|  =  1  iff  C_TP > 1  (λ₁(ℒ_TP) > 0: single trivial constraint)
|C*|  ~  (1 − C_TP)^{−1/2}  as  C_TP → 1^−  (barrier diverges at critical point)
|C*|  → ∞  as  C_TP → 0^+  (present-trapped: no finite barrier suffices)
```

The square-root divergence of |C*| at C_TP = 1 is the **commitment barrier critical exponent** — identical in form to the Ginzburg–Landau order-parameter divergence near a second-order phase transition.

**[D] Commitment Device Taxonomy.** Classical commitment devices in behavioral economics map to barrier structures on ℬ_TP:

| Commitment Device | Barrier Type | Barrier Geometry |

| Retirement account (401k) | Penalty barrier: C adds cost Δ_penalty to crossing 𝒮_PR | Potential energy wall |
| Gym buddy / social contract | Shared barrier: C defined by intersection of two agents' 𝒮_PR | Gallai intersection |

| Mindfulness / present-awareness training | Increases awareness of 𝒮_PR without removing it | Gauge transformation |

## Part VIII — The Self-Control Escape Path

**[D] Definition.** The *self-control path length* B(ℬ_TP) is the minimum number of behavioral decision steps required to move from the hyperbolic basin (C_TP < 1) to the Samuelson basin (C_TP > 1):

```
B(ℬ_TP)  =  min { n ∈ ℕ  :  ∃ (d₁, ..., d_n) s.t. C_TP(b_n) > 1  and  each d_i is accessible from b_{i-1} }
```

**[T] Finite Escape Iff Spectral Gap Positive.** B(ℬ_TP) < ∞ iff there exists a commitment path in ℬ_TP that increases C_TP = ρ/k at each step — i.e., either increases the exponential component ρ (habit formation, increasing patience) or decreases the hyperbolic curvature k (temptation reduction, environment design). The maximum of B(ℬ_TP) over all starting points is the **Bellman escape depth** of the hyperbolic basin.

**[H] Addiction as Fixed Point.** When the hyperbolic curvature k itself increases as a function of reward consumption (k = k(c_t) is state-dependent, with ∂k/∂c > 0 — consuming the addictive good increases present bias), the system develops a stable fixed point at C*_TP < 1. The self-control path must now overcome not just the current hyperbolic curvature but the positive feedback loop that increases k. This creates a limit cycle in the (C_TP, c_t) phase space — a stable non-zero fixed point δ*_PB = (k* − ρ)/k* analogous to the BMST confirmation limit cycle δ*_FI.

**[H] Procrastination as Repeated Renewal.** When the cost of delay is small (V_SS ≈ V_LL, ΔV → 0), the preference reversal delay satisfies D* ~ (ΔV/V_SS)^{2/3}/k → 0. The agent perpetually defers: at each moment, the delay-D task lies inside 𝒮_PR (smaller-sooner wins), and the task is pushed one period forward. This is the **procrastination renewal equation**: the task delay D_n satisfies the recursion D_{n+1} = D_n − 1 + [0 if D_n > D*_n, else 1], which has a stable fixed point at D_∞ = D*_∞ → ∞ as ΔV → 0. Procrastination is not irrationality — it is the stable equilibrium of the hyperbolic discount dynamics when the value surplus ΔV is small.

## Part IX — Open Problems

**[C, TIPS-C1] Preference Reversal Phase Boundary.** The exact equation of the Samuelson–Ainslie critical surface Σ_c ⊂ {(ρ, k, β, V_SS, V_LL, Δ) : D* = 0} is conjectured to satisfy:

```
ρ/k  =  (V_LL/V_SS)^{1/3} · (1 + kΔ)^{−1/3}
```

— a generalized LLD exponent (2/3) in the value ratio, with correction from the delay gap Δ. This would give a precise quantitative boundary between time-consistent and time-inconsistent behavior, experimentally testable via Ainslie-type delay discounting tasks.

**[C, TIPS-C2] Temporal Orchard Farey Density.** The number of discount-visible outcomes in 𝒯 with delay D ≤ N and value V ≤ V_max satisfies the asymptotic:

```
|𝒯 ∩ [0,N] × [0,V_max]|  ~  (6/π²) · C_TP · (1/k) · log(V_max/θ) · N
```

where 6/π² is the Farey density. This would establish a precise Farey-TIPS connection, identifying the temporal capillary length 1/k as the analog of the Farey denominator bound Q in FEST and DKST.

**[C, TIPS-C3] Addiction Limit Cycle Quantization.** For a state-dependent hyperbolic curvature k(c) = k₀(1 + α·c) where c is cumulative consumption and α > 0 is the addiction sensitivity parameter, the stable limit cycle in the (C_TP, c) phase space satisfies:

```
δ*_PB  =  (k* − ρ) / k*  =  α · c* / (1 + α · c*)
```

and the critical addiction onset threshold α_c satisfies a Ginzburg–Landau relation: α_c = ρ/k₀ = C_TP(0). Addiction onset is the crossing of C_TP(0) from above — the same phase transition as the TIPS master equivalence, but now dynamically driven by consumption feedback.

**[C, TIPS-C4] Self-Control Path Length Universality.** The minimum self-control path length from C_TP = c < 1 to C_TP = 1 scales as:

```
B(ℬ_TP)  ~  (1 − c)^{−ν}
```

for a universal exponent ν independent of the specific discount function family (hyperbolic, quasi-hyperbolic, generalized hyperbola). Conjecture: ν = 1/2 (square-root universality class, same as the commitment barrier divergence), arising from the second-order nature of the phase transition at C_TP = 1.

**[C, TIPS-C5] Samuelson Inadmissibility Theorem.** Samuelson himself suspected that no simple discount function could simultaneously satisfy: (i) time consistency, (ii) decreasing impatience (preference reversals decrease as delays lengthen), and (iii) stationarity of risk aversion. Conjecture: conditions (i)–(iii) are jointly realizable iff C_TP = 1 exactly — the Samuelson–Ainslie critical point is the unique consistent-impatient-stationary boundary. For C_TP ≠ 1, at least one of (i)–(iii) fails.
